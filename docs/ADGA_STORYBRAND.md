# ADGA Brand Narrative

This document is the ADGA brand narrative — the underlying story every piece of customer-facing copy should follow. It is a 7-part structure that centers the customer and defines how ADGA shows up to serve them.

Use this as the source of truth for marketing copy, sales decks, onboarding emails, ad creative, landing pages, and any other customer-facing communication. When in doubt, check the copy against the 7 parts. If a piece of copy doesn't reinforce one of these parts, it is probably not helping the brand.

## Positioning Statement

**Designed to help closers move more deals forward — without losing them to slipped follow-ups, scattered tools, or missed handoffs.**

That is the transformation ADGA is designed to deliver. Every piece of customer-facing copy must lead with that transformation — never with what ADGA is or what ADGA was built for.

**The audience:** the closers, dealmakers, operators, producers, rainmakers, and heavy hitters who carry the number. Pro, Team, and Enterprise are tier configurations and only matter on the pricing page.

**Stop saying what we are. Start saying what they get.** Every headline, every subhead, every CTA, every ad must lead with the transformation the closer experiences. The identity words ground the audience.

## Compliance — Never Guarantee Outcomes

**This is non-negotiable and overrides any headline construction.**

We never guarantee specific outcomes, numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, or earnings in customer-facing copy. Doing so is a legal liability.

| Banned | Use Instead |
|---|---|
| "Close 40% more deals" | "Designed to help you close more deals" |
| "Add 6 deals to your quarter" | "Designed to help you add more deals to your pipeline" |
| "$1.2M added to pipeline" | "Designed to help grow your pipeline" |
| "Hit your number every quarter" | "Designed to help you pursue your number" |
| "Guaranteed results" | "Results vary" |

Approved compliance language:
- "Designed to help you..."
- "Built to help you pursue..."
- "Up to..." (only with footnoted source)
- "Results vary"
- "Pursue your goal of..."

Required footer disclaimer on any page that references outcomes: *"Results vary. No specific outcomes are guaranteed."*

**Default voice is the closer working their own book** (the Pro configuration). Every page, every email, every ad speaks to that closer. The team is the natural progression — what the closer grows into when they bring on a teammate. The team is never positioned as a *separate audience* or a *parallel choice*; it is the path the closer is already on.

**The only place tier choice appears is the pricing page.** There, the closer picks whether to start as a Pro, set up a Team workspace, or go Enterprise. Everywhere else, write to the closer and let the team and enterprise paths surface naturally when the closer is ready.

Never write headlines like "Pro or team?" or "for pros and teams" — that is the kind of confusion this document is here to prevent.

## Who ADGA Is For

ADGA is for **closers** — anyone who closes deals professionally. Agency owners. Consultants. Brokers. Advisors. M&A operators. Account executives. Whether they work alone or alongside a team, the identity is the same.

The Pro, Team, and Enterprise tiers are the configurations a closer uses depending on how they work:

| Tier | Configuration | Role in the Narrative |
|---|---|---|
| **Pro** | A closer working their own book of deals | Default voice. Every page speaks to the closer in this configuration |
| **Team** | A team of closers working deals together | Surfaces as the next step the closer grows into. Only appears as a separate choice on the pricing page |
| **Enterprise** | An established team scaled to 15+ closers across offices, with governance and compliance needs | The maturity tier. Same growth arc, larger scale, additional governance needs |

The Pro tier is the entry. The Team tier is the growth. The Enterprise tier is the maturity. One continuous path, not three parallel audiences.

## Headline Construction — Direct-Response Convention

Headlines follow the convention used by the direct-response copywriters in our research archive — Brunson, Kern, Hormozi, Deiss, Halbert, Schwartz, Sugarman, Holmes. These conventions are not optional. Slogans are banned.

### What a real direct-response headline contains

Every headline must have all five elements. Miss any one and it becomes a slogan.

| # | Element | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Curiosity hook** | Opens with a word that creates a knowledge gap: *How · Why · Discover · Revealed · Here's How · The Secret · Inside · What · If You* |
| 2 | **Specific audience qualifier** | Names exactly who this is for using an identity word from the vocabulary tier: closers · dealmakers · operators · producers · rainmakers |
| 3 | **Transformation pointer** | Describes the direction of change *without* guaranteeing a specific outcome: "move more deals forward" · "stop losing deals to slipped follow-ups" · "keep every deal moving" · "run from first signal to repeat purchase" |
| 4 | **Authority / proof element** | "Today's top closers" · "Top dealmakers" · "The same system used by" — borrowed credibility, no fabricated stats |
| 5 | **Objection handler ("without")** | Removes resistance up front: *without hiring another rep · without changing how you sell · without changing how they already close* |

### Length

Real direct-response headlines are **15-30 words.** Short slogans are not headlines. A two-clause "close more, drop fewer" is not a headline.

### Every headline gets a deck (subhead)

Below the headline, a 1-2 sentence subhead expands the transformation, names the audience, and grounds the page. The headline opens the loop. The deck closes it.

## Banned Headline Patterns

Never use any of these:

- **Numeric guarantees** — "Add 6 deals," "Close 40% more," "$1.2M pipeline" — legal liability, no exceptions
- **Two-clause slogans** — "Close more. Drop fewer." (Tagline at best, not a headline)
- **Naked outcomes** — "Move more deals forward." (Missing curiosity hook, authority, audience qualifier)
- **Imperatives without construction** — "Stop losing deals to slipped follow-ups." (Imperative, no curiosity, no audience qualifier — not a headline)
- **"Built for X" / "Made for X"** — says what we built, not what they get
- **Short punchy taglines** — "Where closers close more." (Slogan)
- **Anything under 12 words at the headline level**

## How to Use Each Part

### Character — Who you're writing for

Every page speaks to **the closer** — defaulting to the closer in the Pro configuration (working their own book). Never write to "users," never to "businesses," never to "everyone with a pipeline." The identity word is **closer**. The tier configuration (Pro / Team / Enterprise) is only relevant on the pricing page.

When the page is the pricing page, the closer picks whether to start as a Pro, set up a Team workspace, or go Enterprise. That choice is offered there, and only there.

Everywhere else, the team appears as the closer's natural growth path — not as a parallel audience. Examples:

- Homepage: "Built for closers. Close more deals. When you bring on a teammate, ADGA scales with you."
- Product page: speak to the closer running deals; later in the page, show how the team workspace handles handoffs *when the closer is ready to invite their first teammate*
- Onboarding emails 1-3: the closer is alone; team-focused content triggers when they invite a teammate
- Sales deck: open with the closer's pain → show the team growth path → close with Enterprise scale

When copy drifts to "users" or "anyone in sales" or "for pros and teams," pull it back to **the closer**.

### Problem — What to lead with

Lead with the **external** problem in headlines (concrete, visible — scattered deals, dropped follow-up). Reference the **internal** feeling in subheadings (anxiety, overwhelm). The **philosophical** problem is the bridge that justifies the whole product ("running a real business shouldn't require sticky notes").

The order matters: external grabs attention, internal builds connection, philosophical justifies the cost.

### Guide — How ADGA shows up

ADGA is the platform, never the closer. The closer closes the deal — alone, with a team, or across an enterprise. ADGA gives them the structure to do it. In copy, ADGA does two things:

- **Empathy**: "we've been there" — specific examples of the pain
- **Authority**: "we built ADGA around the actual nine-stage path every B2B deal runs"

Avoid puffery for ADGA ("the best CRM," "the most powerful platform"). Use service language ("built for closers," "the platform that runs the process for you").

### Plan — The 3-step path

Use the 3-step plan whenever the customer needs to know how to start. It must always be three steps. Not four. Not seven. Three.

Vary the wording across surfaces so it doesn't feel formulaic:

- Marketing page: Import / Run / Close
- Onboarding email: Bring your deals in · Run the process · Watch deals close
- Pricing page: Pick a plan · Open the workspace · Run real deals

### Call to Action — Primary and Secondary

Every page has one **primary CTA** (the direct action — Start closing deals) and one **secondary CTA** (the lower-commitment alternative — See the process, Watch a walkthrough).

Never offer three CTAs. Never make the primary CTA vague ("Learn more"). The primary CTA is always a verb that names the outcome.

### Avoid Failure — Stakes

Use sparingly. Not every section needs stakes. Reserve Avoid Failure for moments where the prospect needs to feel why they should act now:

- The opening of a long-form page
- The end of a sales sequence email
- The pre-CTA section on the pricing page

If Avoid Failure is everywhere, it stops landing. Use it 1-2 times per page maximum.

### Success — The after-picture

Paint the after-picture explicitly. Don't assume the prospect will imagine what success looks like — tell them. Use concrete language:

- ❌ "Grow your business"
- ✅ "Every deal in one place. You see what's closing this month with confidence."

Success copy should be specific enough that the prospect can picture their actual day-to-day after using ADGA.

## Voice and Tone Rules

| Rule | Example |
|---|---|
| Second person, always | "You close deals" — not "users close deals" |
| Active voice | "ADGA guides every deal" — not "deals are guided by ADGA" |
| Specific over vague | "Every deal in one place with a clear next action" — not "Better organization" |
| Outcome over feature | "Sleep through the night" — not "Workflow automation engine" |
| Plain English | "Close more, lose fewer" — not "Optimize pipeline velocity" |
| One idea per sentence | Break long sentences in two |
| Confident, not arrogant | "Built for closers" — not "The only platform that matters" |

## Banned Phrases

These phrases break the narrative and should be removed wherever they appear:

- Mythology / framework jargon — never used in ADGA copy. The pro is the pro. The team is the team. No metaphors borrowed from storytelling frameworks.
- "Agentic deal execution platform" — internal language, not customer language
- "Workflow automation" — feature soup, not transformation
- "AI-powered" — generic, says nothing specific
- "Best-in-class" — empty superlative
- "Take your business to the next level" — clichéd, says nothing
- "Empower your team" — corporate filler
- "Revolutionary platform" — hype, no proof
- "Solo" — never used. A closer is a closer whether they work alone or with a team
- "Firm" — never used. The tier names are Pro, Team, and Enterprise. There is no "Firm"
- **"For pros and teams"** / **"Pro or team?"** / any framing that asks the customer to pick — confusing. Default voice is the closer. Pricing page is the only place the tier choice appears
- **"Two heroes"** / **"two audiences"** / **"two customers"** — there is one customer (the closer) on a growth path. Don't fragment them
- Any sentence longer than 22 words

## Checking Copy Against the Narrative

Before publishing any customer-facing copy, run it through this checklist:

- [ ] Is the pro clearly the customer being addressed (default voice)?
- [ ] If the team is mentioned, is it shown as the pro's growth path — not as a parallel audience or a "pro or team?" choice?
- [ ] Is this the pricing page? If yes, both tiers can appear as a choice. If no, only the pro voice should be present.
- [ ] Is at least one Problem (external, internal, or philosophical) named?
- [ ] Does ADGA show up with empathy + authority, not as the one closing the deal?
- [ ] Is there a clear 3-step Plan visible somewhere on the page?
- [ ] Is there exactly one Primary CTA?
- [ ] Is the Success state specific enough to picture?
- [ ] Is Avoid Failure used (if at all) only at moments of decision?
- [ ] Is every sentence in second person, active voice, plain English?
- [ ] Are any banned phrases present?
- [ ] If the page is for one customer only (pro or team), is the other acknowledged elsewhere (e.g., in the upgrade path)?

If any answer is wrong, the copy needs to be revised before it ships.
